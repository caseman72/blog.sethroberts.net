]]> By: Chris MacDonald http://blog.sethroberts.net/2012/08/09/tyler-cowens-unusual-final-exam/#comment-998843 Mon, 13 Aug 2012 14:17:41 +0000 http://blog.sethroberts.net/?p=7322#comment-998843 One result of this strategy is that every student will be correct in their prediction of what will be on the exam. Regardless of which material is actually most important, if a student believes X, Y, and Z will be on the exam, she will study X, Y, and Z. Faced with an open-ended exam, she will write on what she has studied, namely X, Y, and Z.

Or, in principle, another thing that might happen is that a student might write and answer a bunch of suitably difficult questions all rooted in materials from the last half of the course. That would lead to a high grade, despite ignoring (and perhaps being ignorant of) half the material. So, yes, additional constraints would be wise.
